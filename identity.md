## Identity

This section focuses on identity, identity lifecycle management, least privilege, multi-factor authentication, access control, password management and conditional access.

---

## What is considered an identity and what is lifecycle management? 

An identity is an account within an Identity Provider system (Microsoft, Google, etc.) that is linked to a user or asset that can request access to internal systems and applications within the environment. 

Identity lifecycle management refers to steps that control that account's creation, access, changes and removal. 

---

## Real-World application

Imagine you join an organization as a Junior Systems Administrator. The company uses Google as its identity provider. During onboarding, you’re given a unique account that grants access to the systems you wll need daily. When you first log in, you notice you only have access to a few systems, and you cannot make any changes or edits.
In simple terms - your Google account is your identity. Your access is limited according to the least privilege principle, meaning you only have the permissions necessary to perform your job.

Fast forward a year. You’ve been promoted to Senior Systems Administrator. The onboarding team updates your role in the internal systems, which automatically adjusts the systems and permissions you can access. This evolution of roles and access is a real-world example of identity lifecycle management. 

Fast forward five years. A new opportunity comes your way and you separate from the organization. During the offboarding process, your unique account gets disabled, which revokes your access to internal systems. 
This final step completes the full identity lifecycle, from account creation and role-based access, through your role change and promotion, to eventual deactivation when you leave the organization.
